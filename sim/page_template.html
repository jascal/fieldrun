Meet the Threx

The Threx are blind foragers in the dark of an alien sea. They never see one another, so everything they coordinate — finding food, warning of danger, splitting a catch — travels as short pulse-strings of sound: a call. Calls are terse and grammatical, because a wasted pulse is energy a Threx does not have. Their whole language is just 25 pulses:

The corpus and the phrasebook

We generate a big pile of Threx calls — the model’s whole world. Here is a slice of it:

Alongside the model we also build a phrasebook: the strongest pure-memorisation baseline we can. It is just counts — “after this short run of pulses, here is what usually comes next” — plus a recency list and a copy rule. It does no reasoning, only lookup. The trick that powers everything below is one question asked of every pulse the model produces: would the phrasebook, on its own, have produced the same pulse? If yes, the model added nothing — that pulse is retrieved or selected. If the pulse is in no list the phrasebook could produce, the model must have composed it.

What you just watched

Three pulses, three different machines. The danger cry is pure RETRIEVED recall — the phrasebook alone produces it. Choosing the prey for a place is SELECTED — the menu is remembered, context picks from it. And triangulating prey from two currents is COMPOSED — the answer is named nowhere in the call and was never drilled for that exact pair, so no lookup, copy, or n-gram offers it. The model adds the two current-strengths and works it out. That computed slice is the small, hard, irreducible part — and it is exactly where a model stops remembering and starts thinking.
